Lesley Adjunct Faculty are Forming a Union

Why are we choosing to organize?

We teach because we love to. We believe Adjunct Faculty bring a wealth of dedication, field and academic experience to the classroom. It's time for the playing field to level out.
There is an epidemic in higher education, and the solution has been to rely more and more on the part-time faculty contingent labor pool. Adjuncts tend to work in multiple institutions (some times 3 or more) to make rent, pay bills and pay for health insurance at high costs.

An adjunct professor must teach between 12 and 44 classes a year to afford rent and utilities in Boston.

- The High Cost of Adjunct Living: Boston - Adjunct Action

What are the issues surrounding Adjunct Faculty?

  1. Contracts for employment with negotiated and guaranteed wages

    2. As part-time faculty, we are employees at will with the university. We do not know if we will continue to teach from semester to semester, thus making financial and academic planning erratic and unpredictable. In many cases, part-time faculty are asked informally and in the last days before a semester starts to teach, again allowing for little course planning, and resulting in a decreased student experience.

  2. Fair wages, and the elimination of half-wage compensation for under-enrolled courses

    3. An official practice at the university around part-time faculty compensation, surrounds the notion that faculty are told they will receive full wages for a course taught for 15 weeks, yet when a course becomes under-enrolled, faculty are forced to take a half-pay cut, or are paid on a per-student basis. We feel this is unfair, as we continue to teach the full course, for the entire semester, and it is not our issue if enrollments are not met, and it is not our responsibility to market courses. - Median Wages at Lesley University

  3. Recognition that contingent faculty make up more than 65% of the faculty pool

    4. Adjunct Support is Student Success. Currently, the average ratio of part-time to full time faculty teaching is nearing 70% part-time, and 30% full time. On top of this, the availability of full time opportunities has dwindled to almost none. The university is relying on the backs of part-time faculty to lift the brunt of teaching loads, and taking advantage of the fact the part-time teachers teach because they love to. We have worked this way for so long because of our love for teaching, but are ready for the recognition we deserve by means of a voice in curriculum steering, development and more a more leveled professional playing field.

  4. Access to health and savings benefit packages

    5. Because of our contingent status as Adjunct teachers, we have zero access to health insurance or savings/retirement planning through the university. We believe that if we continue to be the faculty majority, we should have access to some form of health and financial planning benefits. The more towards some form of benefit security we can push for, the stronger the faculty can become.
